The Case for Synthetic Hydrocarbons

March 2025

Here's the one-line case for synthetic hydrocarbons:

It's the only renewable energy carrier that can be implemented fast enough to avoid the worst effects of climate change without derailing human progress.

Our entire world runs on fossil fuels. The tremendous progress of human civilization over the past 100 years has come as a direct consequence of our ability to dig up and use biomass stored in the Earth's crust in the form of coal, oil, and natural gas. All living organisms are composed of hydrocarbons, and it's the plant/animal matter from prehistoric times that accumulated in deposits across the planet from millions of years of pressurization, heating, and sedimentation—hence the name fossil fuels.

Quick chemistry discussion: Hydrocarbons are a highly reduced form of carbon. In the presence of oxygen and a little bit of activation energy, oxygen rips off and replaces the hydrogen, oxidizing the carbon in the process. This particular redox reaction, known as combustion, converts hydrocarbons and oxygen into carbon dioxide and water, releasing significant amounts of energy that is then available to do work.

CH4 + 2O2 → CO2 + 2H2O (ΔHC˚ = -891 kJ/mol)

Combustion of methane (CH4), the main component of natural gas

The negative heat of combustion indicates that the products (CO2 and H2O) are lower in energy than the reactants (CH4 and O2), known as an exothermic reaction. Hydrocarbons are essentially solar energy stored in chemical bonds (the biomass when it was alive initially got its energy from photosynthesis). Through drilling and fracking, we exposed these hydrocarbons to oxygen (readily available in our atmosphere), releasing the stored solar energy. This has been an incredible boon. Fossil fuel combustion spins turbines to provide electricity, powers ICEs to transport people and goods, and heats our homes and industries. There are also many non-combustion use cases for fossil fuels, including as reducing agents to process metal oxides into the critical metals we use every day and as chemical feedstocks for plastics, lubricants, adhesives, asphalt, dyes, detergents, and many more products upon which we rely.

Fossil fuels have lifted many people out of poverty and produced incredible wealth that would have been inconceivable centuries and even decades ago. Rightly, petroleum discovery and utilization are considered one of the greatest engineering achievements of the 20th century.

There's just one problem: they're finite resources whose use drastically impacts the Earth.

This conflict between the incredible bounties of fossil fuels and their detrimental effects on planetary systems is one of the defining challenges of our era. Atmospheric carbon dioxide has been steadily rising since our use of fossil fuels, and its propensity to trap heat on the Earth's surface has caused a corresponding increase in global average surface temperature. While it's unclear how exactly this temperature increase will manifest, we do know that it's causing an imbalance in our geosystem that has led to rising sea levels, more natural disasters, and decreased biodiversity, among other issues. And because these systems are interlinked, we're observing a snowball effect that punishes the poorest people in the world who cannot adapt.

Furthermore, fossil fuels are inherently limited resources. While estimates for years of global fossil fuel reserves left vary, current predictions give oil and gas about 60 years and coal about 150 years. Although, to be fair, we have historically been quite poor at estimating reserves. Nevertheless, the point remains that the fuels upon which we so heavily depend will run out sometime in the future, at which point we will need to transition anyway. Why not do it now when that transition could also save the climate?

Given these effects, many policymakers have proposed rapid decarbonization as an uncomfortable but necessary means to mitigate climate change. The inconvenient truth that gets left out in these discussions, however, is the massive impoverishment such climate policies would induce. Our society's entire industrial stack has been designed for hydrocarbons, so artificially constraining their use via taxes and regulation would have immeasurable downstream effects on economic productivity. Even if we assume that the richest countries can afford to limit fossil fuel usage, it is morally corrupt to deny developing nations a chance at building wealth through a hydrocarbon-based economy. Those who suggest that we can simply switch off fossil fuels quickly have a tenuous grasp of the role hydrocarbons play in our society and the economic benefit of leveraging cheap energy.

This problem would be unsolvable if it weren't for one important development: the rapid cost decline of solar photovoltaics (PV).

Fossil fuels are no longer the cheapest way to obtain energy; that title belongs to solar PV and wind. More importantly, solar PV will experience swift price decreases from innovation on cells, manufacturing, and installation, while fossil fuels will slowly increase in cost as supply dwindles and convenient reserves are tapped out. We're already seeing this play out with accelerating installed capacity: in 2024, we added solar PV at a rate of 1 MW every minute!

End use of solar electricity will no doubt be an important part of the future, but the most impactful role solar will play will be converting its low-cost, green electrons into value-added products that can be easily integrated into our existing industrial stack. The most important among these products is hydrocarbons.

Similar to how fossil fuels are solar energy stored in chemical bonds, we can run the reverse combustion process to convert CO2 and water into hydrocarbons and oxygen by supplying energy from solar. This is how plants work, although there are many other catalytic pathways to achieving this overall reaction!

This isn't a novel idea, but historically, the challenge with synthesizing hydrocarbons this way has been that the intense energy requirements could not be satisfied in a way that was economically competitive with fracking. This process is only about 25-30% efficient, meaning most of the inputted solar energy gets wasted as heat. The key unlock now is that solar is rapidly approaching the cost threshold (~$10/MWh) when this process would be economically competitive. Essentially, the gap between the price of solar and a comparable unit of oil/natural gas needs to be large enough such that the cost hit of lossily converting this cheap electricity into hydrocarbons is negligible. This is a tall task given how inexpensive fracking is, but solar is on its way. The right way for this kind of process (or any commodity production) to take maximum advantage of cheap solar is to minimize CAPEX and adapt to the intermittency. Margins are brutal for commodities, so they have no room for extraneous costs. Batteries are too expensive to justify the extra runtime (the final product is too cheap to recoup costs), and the lower efficiency from a low-CAPEX process is well worth the cost savings.

FAQs:

How is this carbon neutral?

All the carbon that goes into synthetic fuels comes from the atmosphere. When these fuels are then combusted, they return the same amount of carbon in the form of carbon dioxide back into the atmosphere, completing the cycle. The problem with fossil fuels is that they take carbon that was stored in the Earth's crust and put it into the atmosphere with no way of capturing that carbon dioxide. This net flux causes the atmospheric carbon dioxide accumulation we see today. In the case of synthetic fuels, no new carbon is released to the air.

If converting solar into hydrocarbons is inefficient, why not directly use the electricity?

Electricity is a higher-grade energy source than chemical fuels, so we will eventually electrify many of our current energy use cases. However, this will take lots of time and innovation since electrification needs to beat hydrocarbon-based processes that have been optimized over the past century. Even if all end uses were to be magically electrified tomorrow, that still doesn't address the fossil fuel-powered uses that already exist. These legacy processes, along with hard-to-electrify sectors, will need a carbon-neutral fuel.

We have much better infrastructure to store, use, and transport hydrocarbons than electricity itself. Power grids are notoriously difficult and expensive to build out. Instead of changing the countless end uses of energy, synthetic hydrocarbons can be easily dropped into existing infrastructure and automatically make every end use carbon-neutral. An interesting corollary of this setup is that gas pipelines and oil tankers would effectively serve as batteries, allowing intermittent solar to be cheaply stored. Lithium-ion batteries are obviously more efficient and will be cheap, but it's convenient to have a backwards-compatible fuel that does not depend on massive upgrades and expansion to a weak grid.

Why not store the captured CO2 instead of wasting energy to convert it to fuel?

From an environmental perspective, this idea makes sense, and indeed, we will likely need carbon capture and storage as long as we keep using fossil fuels. The challenge is economic since CO2 itself has no intrinsic utility (hence why we dispose it into the atmosphere for free). Carbon markets are attempts at artificially injecting value, but not only are they difficult to regulate, they will become obsolete if there's a more economically productive thing to do with the captured carbon.

Why not hydrogen or other energy carriers?

Again, infrastructure. While it is true that solar energy can be stored in different chemical bonds, we already know how to handle hydrocarbons as well as any other molecule. Hydrogen is extremely challenging to store and transport, and ammonia is toxic. All our end uses are expecting hydrocarbons anyway, so why not supply that?

Will this ruin the balance of CO2?

This is a bit more forward-thinking concern. If synthetic hydrocarbons were to completely replace fossil fuels, then the atmospheric CO2 concentration would start to decrease, since some percentage of the carbon will turn into plastics, adhesives, lubricants, etc., that won't return to the atmosphere as carbon dioxide again. The planet needs some amount of carbon dioxide to retain heat and prevent an ice age, so technically we do run the risk of having too little CO2 in the atmosphere if this process were to scale. Pre-industrial concentration of CO2 was around 280-300 ppm (right now we're at 420 ppm), so in this hypothetical future, we could monitor levels until we reach this threshold, at which point we would either phase out these solar-to-fuels plants or encourage fossil fuel use. What a problem that would be!

What markets would benefit the most from synthetic hydrocarbons?

The short answer is any sector that relies on them today because they aren't easily electrified. I think the most important of these is aviation. Small-distance vehicles like cars and buses will reasonably be electrified in the future, but flight specifically has much to gain from having cheap access to chemically dense fuels. Weight and volume are at a premium in the aviation industry, meaning each unit of your fuel needs to carry more bang for its buck. Kerosene has an energy density of 43 MJ/kg, whereas lithium-ion batteries have an energy density of ~1 MJ/kg. Even with the 2x efficiency boost from using an electric motor versus a gas turbine, batteries will struggle to compete with traditional jet fuel. Hydrogen has great gravimetric density but terrible volumetric density, meaning that it too won't be able to replace long-distance aviation. Sustainable aviation fuels (SAFs) that are compatible with existing jet engines will be the only reasonable way to "decarbonize" all aviation.

Hydrocarbons are not inherently bad; we just need to close the carbon cycle instead of letting carbon dioxide build up in the atmosphere. Fuels synthesized from cheap solar power will allow us to continue progressing as a civilization without making the planet uninhabitable. Solar resources are much more distributed across the world as compared to fossil fuels, meaning nations could power their own industries without relying as heavily on foreign imports. This has massive consequences for geopolitics and international trade, as wars that are fought over energy resources would diminish.

Our society is at its best when we let the invisible hand of free markets drive progress, as opposed to regulations, subsidies, and taxes that artificially constrain competition. The answer to climate change is not making hydrocarbons more expensive to use through regulation, but less expensive to use through technological innovation. Within the next 5 years, solar will reach the point when fuels synthesized from its energy will be cheaper than their fossil counterparts, delivering a green dividend that continues to grow human prosperity. By then, we'll consider it barbaric that we even dug holes in the ground to get something as fundamental as energy.
